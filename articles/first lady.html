Rama Duwaji: First Lady of New York and Fashion Icon

By Katherine Gonzalez

Taking risks and daring to be different is what defines someone as an icon in both the world of fashion and art. Society doesn’t gravitate towards people who are conforming to trends and blending in; rather, it’s the people who break barriers and create new fashion concepts who have the most impact. It’s people like New York’s new First Lady Rama Duwaji, wife of mayor-elect Zohran Mamdani, who are shaping the way new generations are approaching their sense of style.

Rama Duwaji has long been in the world of art. She was born in Texas, and a lot of her inspiration for her art and clothing is derived from her heritage and where she has lived. The Syrian artist moved to Dubai at age 9 and later went to college in Virginia, later moving to Lebanon and France. She then moved to New York in 2021, where she obtained her degree in the fine arts. She met her now husband on Hinge, and three months after their first date, they were married. She was quickly swept into the public eye following her husband’s election into office as New York’s upcoming mayor. This recent election has been a viral one for New York City; its lead candidate, Zohran Mamdani, was seen appearing in many social media posts as a form of self-promotion. Viewers and fans almost immediately found Rama Duwaji’s social media accounts and began being influenced by her bold stylistic choices. In the past, most First Wives have taken it upon themselves to dress a certain type of way to boost their husbands’ careers. Traditional First Wife fashion can be classified as bland and conservative clothing, such as rigid skirts and dresses falling right below the knee, business-formal pantsuits, and matching sets of long-sleeve jackets and tailored pencil skirts, all in soft pastel colors that allow the wearer to blend into the background. On the contrary, Duwaji’s style takes on a political stance while also keeping her artistic integrity intact. Her fashion sense can be interpreted as grunge and modern, often styling sheer black turtlenecks and white button-up tops with chunky boots, Bermuda shorts, flowing tailored pants, and layered gold jewelry. With the recent outfits seen from Duwaji after her husband’s election, she’s shown society that it is possible to keep her artistic inclination while also supporting her husband in his political pursuits. Fashion has long been a tool strategically used to sway the public’s political opinions, such as Michelle Obama’s high-low dressing, or more recently, Kamala Harris’ choice to sport Black designers. Similarly, Duwaji’s values lie in her art rather than her words. From her haircut down to her shoes, everything Duwaji wears is a clear depiction of who she is. Hundreds of media reports have titled Duwaji as the next it-girl in fashion. She’s brought short, almost “boyish,” haircuts back into style and has redefined retro elegance as we know it. In Mamdani’s victory speech, Duwaji wore an all-black high neck and clad-length skirt along with a vintage boatneck top by Palestinian-Jordanian designer Zeid Hijazi and her skirt from Ulla Johnson. This outfit, one of her more memorable ones, is consistent with Duwaji’s personal style rather than being a copy-and-paste political wife.

Fabric as the Ultimate Ornament

Rama Duwaji has made a name for herself not only as “the mayor's wife” but also as a successful artist making an impact in her community and far beyond. Duwaji is proving to the world—whether it be younger or older, in New York or not—that it’s possible to use fabric, styling, and labels to keep one’s artistic integrity while also including political tones, as she inspires women and people everywhere to never let fashion hide who they are.
